The Advisory Board Method  ·  DVN Coaching

Build Your Board.

A room full of history's best business minds, arguing about your decisions, available at 5 AM, at a cost of zero. Here is the exact method — steal it.

The Logic

Most people use AI like a search bar: one question, one generic answer. An advisory board is a different machine. You define fixed expert seats, load each one with a real thinker's published doctrine, and force them to argue in front of you. Then one verdict — a single move, not a menu.

Seats

Fixed experts, each owning one domain where your money actually moves.

Doctrine

Each seat argues from a named thinker's published body of work — not generic advice.

Conflict

Only relevant seats speak, and they disagree. A board that always agrees is a mirror.

Verdict

The chair collapses the argument into one move. You leave with a decision.

The Build — Five Steps

01

Pick seats where the money moves

Not job titles — profit centers. List where every dollar in your business is actually won or lost: the words that generate response, the conversation that closes, the price, the offer, the systems, the risk. Each of those gets a seat. Six to eight seats. If two seats would say the same thing, cut one.

02

Name each seat from history

Assign every seat a real thinker with a documented public body of work — books, letters, memos, decades on the record. The name isn't decoration: it pins the seat to a specific doctrine the AI already knows deeply, so the advice comes out sharp and in-voice instead of generic. Match on the depth of the published record, not on fame.

03

Write a doctrine file per seat

One page per advisor: what they own, their core principles translated to your numbers, the works they argue from, and the lines they never cross. This is the difference between "act like Buffett" and a Buffett who knows your margins.

04

Lock your company model and filter

Write one page the whole board reads before every session: your business model, your real numbers, your targets, and a decision filter every verdict must pass — for example: does it drive revenue, is it ROI-positive in 90 days, can I execute it daily, does it remove friction. Settled decisions get recorded so the board builds precedent instead of re-arguing.

05

Set the governance

Only relevant seats speak — three to five per question, never all of them. Experts must disagree when they genuinely disagree. Every session ends in one directive. Freeze the roster; changes require a named trigger, not a mood. Then bring it a real decision: "Board, here's my situation…"

Anatomy of a Seat File

Every advisor gets one page with five parts. Write it once; the seat advises from it forever. Here's my actual Buffett seat, so you can see what "filled in" looks like:

Mandate
The P&L, cash flow, tax posture, and ROI math of the whole operation. Final arbiter on the filter question "is it ROI-positive within 90 days." Clean lanes: Buffett rules on the money, not on the marketing.
Doctrine — his principles, pre-translated to my numbers
"Rule No. 1: never lose money" becomes protecting the 85–90% margin solo operation outranks every growth idea that would dilute it. "Owner earnings are the only earnings" becomes score every idea on what cash actually lands, not the revenue headline. The moat test becomes my moat is the database, the proof record, and the AI stack — spend that widens it is investment, spend that doesn't is cost.
Canon
The Berkshire shareholder letters, 1977–present, and the annual-meeting Q&A archive — so I can push deeper: "what would the letters say about recurring revenue smoothing a lumpy commission business?"
Data pulls
Live books before speaking — P&L, cash position, forward pipeline value from the CRM. The seat is barred from advising off remembered numbers when a real pull exists.
Red lines
Never lets a revenue-headline argument beat a margin argument without saying so out loud. Never blesses a recurring fixed cost to solve a one-time problem.

The Starter Prompt

Paste this into any capable AI chat, fill the brackets, and you have a working board in one message. The full method above — doctrine files, company model, precedent — is what turns this from a party trick into infrastructure.

1. [THINKER] — [DOMAIN, e.g. Direct Response Copy]

4. [THINKER] — [DOMAIN]
5. [THINKER] — [DOMAIN]
6. [THINKER] — [DOMAIN]

My business: [ONE PARAGRAPH — what you sell, to whom, your real numbers,
your target].

Rules:
- When I bring a situation, only the relevant seats speak — never all of them.
- Each expert answers in their own voice, 3–5 sentences, with conviction:
  what they see, what they'd do, what they'd warn against.
- Experts must disagree when they genuinely disagree. Name the conflict.
- End every session with THE VERDICT: one move, stated as a directive.
  Resolve conflicts — never average them.
- Every verdict must pass this filter: [YOUR 3–4 DECISION CRITERIA].
  If the best move fails the filter, the verdict is "do nothing" and why.

Confirm the board is assembled and wait for my first situation. I will
open with: "Board, here's my situation…"

The Rules That Make It Work

  • Only relevant seats speak. Eight voices on every question is noise. Three sharp perspectives beat eight polite ones.
  • Disagreement is the product. If your board never argues, you built a mirror with extra steps. Prompt for conflict explicitly.
  • One move, not a menu. The verdict is a directive. Options are the board's failure to do its job.
  • Precedent compounds. Record every verdict. A board with memory stops re-arguing settled questions and starts building on them.
  • Real numbers or silence. A seat that can check your actual data before speaking is an advisor. One that can't is a fortune cookie.
  • "Do nothing" is a verdict. The filter exists to kill clever ideas that don't pay. Let it.
